Survey experiment · Qandy Qantar · n≈804

Securitisation framing & police support

A pre-registered survey experiment studying how securitisation and human rights framing affect support for police violence in Kazakhstan after the 2022 Qandy Qantar events. The key finding is a null: securitisation framing did not increase trust in law enforcement, suggesting diminishing returns from authoritarian messaging. We also found domain-specific attitudes toward police force, with greater approval of repression against feminist protests than labor protests.

Constructivist grounded theory · 808 survey responses · 5 interviews

Visible Threats, Invisible Lives: Meaning-Making of Sexual and Gender Minorities in Qazaqstan

A constructivist grounded theory study of how Qazaqstanis make sense of sexual and gender minorities (SGM). We analyse 808 open-ended survey responses and five follow-up interviews and identify four frameworks organised around visibility management: threat narratives linking queer visibility to moral and national decline; conditional tolerance that holds as long as sexual minorities remain invisible; strategic disengagement through claimed unfamiliarity or silence; and acceptance grounded in compassion, personal connections, or individual rights.

Language strongly shapes which framework respondents reach for. 71% of Qazaq speakers expressed negative attitudes compared to 37% of Russian speakers, with Qazaq speakers more often drawing on religious and family narratives and Russian speakers on privacy and individual rights discourses. We also show that large-scale survey corpora can support grounded theory when interview recruitment proves difficult, and we present the resultant grounded theory in line with calls for greater transparency in CGT research.
