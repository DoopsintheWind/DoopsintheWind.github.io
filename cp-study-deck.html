A preregistered study of learning with AI · Fall 2026

Cognitive
Partnership.

What kind of AI co-working leaves students understanding more without AI?

Dinara Pisareva · Nazarbayev University · Principal Investigator
Denis de Crombrugghe · Narxoz Business School · Co-Investigator

02
Start with one student

The semester went well. The grades are good. Now close the laptop.

  • Picture a student at semester's end: every essay polished with AI, every reading summarised by AI, strong grades throughout.
  • Now ask them to explain their own argument with the laptop closed.
  • Sometimes the knowledge is in their head — and sometimes it was living in the chat window, and it left when the AI did.
  • This study is about telling those two students apart.
03
Why this is hard to see

Two things can be true at the same time

  • Working with AI feels transformative: in a three-wave study of 912 students, handing tasks to AI positively predicted self-reported transformative learning (Wang & Zhang, 2026).
  • But their outcome is self-report — and perceived learning cannot detect erosion: a student can sincerely report transformation while unaided capability declines.
  • Both findings can hold at once: heavy delegation that feels like growth, and a shrinking capacity to work alone.
  • Adjudicating between them requires measuring the student with the AI removed.
04
What research already knows

Handing work to a tool is usually smart — until it isn't

  • Cognitive offloading: distributing mental work onto external tools — a shopping list carrying your memory.
  • When people expect a computer to store information, they recall less of the content and more about where to find it (Sparrow, Liu & Wegner, 2011).
  • Offloading is usually adaptive: it frees limited cognitive resources for harder work (Risko & Gilbert, 2016).
  • It harms learning under one condition: when it discharges work germane to the learning goal and the freed effort is not reinvested — offload the formatting, fine; offload the understanding, that's the loss.
05
The key concept

Cognitive residue: what stays when the tool is gone

  • Salomon, Perkins & Globerson (1991) distinguish effects with technology — performance while the tool is in hand — from effects of it: what remains once it is gone.
  • That remainder is cognitive residue — for educators, the outcome that matters.
  • The question is not what a student can do while an AI assists, but what they can reconstruct unaided.
  • Residue accrues only under mindful, effortful engagement — it is earned, not absorbed.
06
The idea we're testing

Cognitive partnership

Sustained intellectual work with AI as a genuine partner in a shared system of thinking.

  • The idea is grounded in the distributed cognition literature (Hollan, Hutchins & Kirsh, 2000): thinking spreads across persons, tools, and settings — the functional system, not the individual, is the unit of analysis.
  • When student and AI work well together, the thinking happens in that configuration, and the product belongs to it.
  • An alternative position to "AI as a tool": sustained co-work, with the understanding retained by the student.
07
What partnership is made of

Three dimensions, growing from one precondition

Teaching cannot act on the student–AI configuration itself, but it can develop what the student brings to it, so we measure the student-side orientation — three co-equal dimensions:

Purpose

Working with AI to think further, not to finish faster.

Engagement

Bringing curiosity and imagination — trying things you hadn't thought to try.

Iteration

Pushing back and building over many rounds, not taking the first answer.

Ontological openness — staying open about what AI is — is the enabling antecedent: taught and measured, but not a facet of the orientation.

08
Dimension one

Purpose: think further, not finish faster

  • The usual story about AI is productivity — but universities are not in the time-saving business.
  • The prize is a mind that reaches further than it could alone.
  • Partnership purpose: offload the trivial, keep the core — the student can explain the whole logic without the AI in the room.
  • Handing over the understanding itself is over-reliance, the failure mode we want to minimise.

"My goal with AI is to think further than I could alone."

09
Dimension two

Engagement: curiosity and imagination do the reaching

  • Purpose sets the destination; curiosity and imagination do the travelling.
  • Without them the partnership flattens into transactions: ask, receive, submit.
  • With them, an odd answer becomes a new direction — the student ends up somewhere neither party would have gone alone.
  • Trait curiosity predicts AI self-efficacy, which predicts use (Schutte & Li, 2025); whether engagement predicts lasting knowledge is what we test.

"I work with AI to come up with options I wouldn't have thought of alone."

10
Dimension three

Iteration: the partnership lives in the back-and-forth

  • A single exchange with AI is retrieval, not partnership.
  • Taking the first answer and stopping is over-reliance wearing a friendly face.
  • The partnership lives in the loop — push back, redirect, build — and the loop is where the work stays yours.
  • Iteration is also behaviourally observable: students' AI process logs let self-reports be checked against actual practice.

"My best work with AI happens over several rounds, not one."

11
The precondition

Staying open about what AI is

  • To enter a real partnership, a student must grant AI the standing of a partner worth real effort.
  • "It's just a tool" invites the opposite: minimal effort, tasks finished and forgotten.
  • So the courses treat what is AI? as genuinely open — because among researchers, it genuinely is.
  • Presented as one stance among several, never required: students take whatever position they find defensible, and grades never touch it.
  • Ontological openness is the enabling antecedent: taught first, measured alongside the three dimensions, examined separately in the analysis.

"I treat what AI is as an open question, not something already settled."

12
The study

Three real courses, one semester, Fall 2026

A within-person pre/post design embedded in three authentic courses at Nazarbayev University, all taught with the same partnership pedagogy:

CourseWhoAI work students do
Research Methods (PLS210)UndergraduatesWeekly AI conversations; research designs co-built with AI; creative presentation of research projects on GitHub.
AI & Social Science (PLS419/519)Upper-level + MAA semester-long public project built with AI and presented on GitHub, plus two op-eds about AI.
Qualitative Methods (PLS514)Graduate studentsAnalytical portfolios: grounded theory, RTA, and process tracing applied to AI-generated case studies, first without and then with AI, plus a comprehensive comparison of the two passes — differences, commonalities, implications for data interpretation.
  • N = 108 enrolled; analytic sample ≈ 70 after consent filtering and completeness.
  • Regular teaching and assessment constitute the data collection.
13
How we measure

Week 1 and Week 14: the same measures, twice

All measures run in Weeks 1 and 14; every model adjusts for the student's own baseline.

The survey — online, take-home

Twelve self-developed CP items (three per dimension plus the antecedent block), the AIDep-22 Cognitive Dependence subscale (Wu et al., 2026), and an adapted 25-item AI-literacy scale (Lee & Park, 2024).

The quiz — in class, on paper, no AI

Six course-specific items (3 multiple-choice, 3 open-ended) targeting understanding and application, identical across the two waves.

The quiz is the study's objective outcome — the residue measure.

14
The heart of the design

The no-AI test: knowledge you can't google mid-answer

  • Self-report cannot separate learning from perceived learning; an objective no-AI measure can.
  • The items demand reconstruction, not recall: each presents a scenario and asks which reasoning is correct, and why.
  • Administered on paper, in class, both waves; open-ended answers rubric-scored by two raters blind to wave, with inter-rater agreement reported.
  • The two sittings use the same six items — on paper, never returned to students, thirteen weeks apart — so item security holds and practice effects stay bounded.
  • What appears on that sheet is residue: knowledge that survives the AI's removal.
15
Hypotheses

Three confirmatory hypotheses, preregistered on OSF

The predictor is the partnership composite — the mean of purpose, engagement, and iteration; the three tests are Holm-corrected. H1 carries the evidential weight — its outcome is objective; H2 and H3 are self-report and triangulate.

H1 Cognitive residue

Controlling for Week-1 levels, the partnership composite positively predicts no-AI knowledge-test scores at Week 14.

H2 Over-reliance

Controlling for Week-1 levels, the partnership composite negatively predicts cognitive dependence on AI at Week 14.

H3 AI literacy

Controlling for Week-1 levels, the partnership composite positively predicts total AI-literacy scores at Week 14.

Powered at .80 for medium effects (r ≈ .33; .38 at the strictest Holm bar); smaller effects arrive as wide intervals, reported as such. Per-dimension effects and the antecedent→composite link are exploratory.

16
Measurements · the predictor

Measuring the orientation: twelve items, three per block

  • Each dimension is measured by three self-developed 5-point Likert items, one reverse-coded per block; ontological openness has its own three-item block outside the composite.
  • The partnership composite = the mean of the three dimension means, z-scored within course — the sole confirmatory predictor.
  • The items are new, so reliability is estimated from the study data (McDonald's omega and Cronbach's alpha, both waves), with a pre-committed floor: any block below ω = .60 leaves the composite and is reported descriptively.
  • One behavioural validity check: the iteration items are set against iteration markers coded from students' AI process logs — the check that does not rely on self-report.
17
Measurements · the outcomes

Measuring the outcomes: residue, dependence, literacy

H1 No-AI knowledge test

The same six course-specific items at both waves: 3 multiple-choice (0/1) + 3 open-ended scored 0–2 against a rubric by two raters blind to wave; total 0–9, z-scored within course.

H2 Cognitive dependence

The Cognitive Dependence subscale of the AIDep-22 (Wu et al., 2026), five items administered intact.

H3 AI literacy

An adapted 25-item AI-literacy scale (Lee & Park, 2024): the total score is confirmatory; the five subscales are an exploratory breakdown.

Analysis: regression-adjusted change (ANCOVA) with course fixed effects and prior AI use as covariate, robust standard errors; estimation-led reporting with 95% CIs.

18
Doing it honestly

The limitations

The design must answer its own strongest objection: the researcher built the framework, teaches it, and measures it. The answer is architectural.

Preregistered

Hypotheses, instruments, and the full analysis plan are locked on OSF before the first class meets.

Gatekeeping

A co-investigator holds all student responses and consent forms during the semester; the PI does not know who consented into the study until final grades and appeals close.

Blind-scored

Answers are transcribed, wave-stripped, and shuffled before independent rating; agreement statistics and an instructor-only sensitivity analysis are reported.

Participation is voluntary and pseudonymous; consent decisions are invisible to the instructor and cannot touch grades. Consent self-selection likely tilts the sample toward engaged students — range restriction on the predictor, which makes the tests conservative.

19
Why it matters

The question isn't whether students will work with AI

  • That question is settled — students already work with AI, in every discipline.
  • The open question: what kind of working relationship leaves students knowing more once the AI is out of the room?
  • If partnership leaves measurable residue, assessment design gains a testable target: AI-free checkpoints, with/without-AI contrasts, process logs as evidence of engagement.
  • Teaching design gains a template too: creative projects where students build with AI, iterate, and publish — folding the results into a personal GitHub or LinkedIn — so co-working visibly compounds into a trajectory, not just a grade, and the get-the-essay-done mindset loses its grip.
  • A null is equally informative.
