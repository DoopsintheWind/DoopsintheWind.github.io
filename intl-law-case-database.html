Nicaragua v. United States (1986)
Court: ICJ Type: Contentious Citation: ICJ Reports 1986, p. 14
Contentious Customary Law
▾
Facts

Nicaragua brought proceedings against the US for supporting Contra rebels, mining harbors, and conducting direct military operations. The US argued its actions were collective self-defense on behalf of El Salvador.

Legal Issue

Under what circumstances can acts of a non-state armed group be attributed to a state? What standard of control must a state exercise over an armed group for attribution to apply?

Holding

The Court applied the "effective control" test: acts of the Contras were attributable to the US only if the US exercised effective control over the specific operations in which violations occurred. General financing and equipping was insufficient.

Key Reasoning

The Court distinguished between general support (funding, training, equipping) and operational control over specific acts. The former creates responsibility for the act of supporting, but does not make the supported group's acts attributable to the supporting state.

Bosnia v. Serbia (Genocide Convention, 2007)
Court: ICJ Type: Contentious Citation: ICJ Reports 2007, p. 43
Contentious Jus Cogens
▾
Facts

Bosnia brought proceedings against Serbia alleging responsibility for genocide at Srebrenica and elsewhere. Serbia had provided extensive military, financial, and logistical support to Republika Srpska forces (VRS) but argued it did not control specific operations.

Legal Issue

Was Serbia responsible for genocide committed by the VRS at Srebrenica, either through direct attribution or through failure to prevent? Does the Nicaragua "effective control" or the ICTY's "overall control" test apply?

Holding

The ICJ reaffirmed the Nicaragua "effective control" test, rejecting the ICTY's broader "overall control" standard from Tadic. Found Serbia did not exercise effective control over the specific Srebrenica operation, but did violate its obligation to prevent genocide.

Key Reasoning

The Court held that the "overall control" test was developed for different purposes (establishing the international character of a conflict for IHL) and was not appropriate for state responsibility attribution under general international law.

ARSIWA Article 8 — Direction or Control
Source: ILC Draft Articles (2001) Type: Treaty/Codification Status: Not a treaty; annexed to UNGA Res. 56/83
Treaty Customary Law
▾
Provision Text

"The conduct of a person or group of persons shall be considered an act of a State under international law if the person or group of persons is in fact acting on the instructions of, or under the direction or control of, that State in carrying out the conduct."

ILC Commentary Summary

The three disjunctive conditions — instructions, direction, control — represent different modalities but all require a specific factual relationship between the state and the particular conduct. The article is narrower than agency in domestic law.

Relationship to Case Law

Article 8 codifies the Nicaragua "effective control" test. The gap between Art. 8 and the ICTY's "overall control" standard remains the central debate in attribution law.
